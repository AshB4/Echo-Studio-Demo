# I Don't Trust "Because"

I have a habit that probably annoys people.

I don't trust the answer:

> "Because."

I don't mean I think people are lying.

I mean that "because" isn't an explanation.

It's a stopping point.

And I hate stopping points.

As a kid, it sounded like:

> "Because I said so."

As a developer, it sounds like:

> "That's just how the API works."

> "That's how we've always done it."

> "The framework doesn't like that."

> "Facebook won't let you."

Maybe all of those statements are true.

But I still want to know why.

Not because I enjoy arguing.

Because understanding the reason changes what you can do next.

If an API behaves strangely because of rate limits, that's one problem.

If it's because of authentication scopes, that's another.

If it's because someone made a product decision ten years ago and nobody wants to revisit it, that's something else entirely.

The explanation matters.

I think that's why I interrogate code the way I do.

When something breaks, I don't immediately want the fix.

I want the story.

What changed?

Who touched it?

What assumptions existed?

What evidence do we have?

What actually happened?

People sometimes joke that developers just Google everything.

There's truth in that.

But the good ones aren't collecting answers.

They're collecting models.

They want to understand enough that the next weird thing becomes a little less mysterious.

I do this outside of code too.

Marketing.

Workflows.

Social platforms.

Hiring processes.

If something feels repetitive, inefficient, or strange, I start asking questions.

Why is it done this way?

Who benefits from this design?

What problem was this solving originally?

Is that problem still real?

Sometimes the answer is:

> There actually is a good reason.

Sometimes the answer is:

> Nobody knows.

And occasionally the answer is:

> We've just always done it this way.

Those are my favorite.

Because that's where interesting things happen.

Not every system needs to be reinvented.

But every system deserves curiosity.

I think people mistake curiosity for rebellion.

It isn't.

I don't ask questions because I think I'm smarter than everyone else.

I ask questions because I assume there's context I don't have yet.

And once I understand the context, I can make better decisions.

Maybe I follow the rule.

Maybe I work around it.

Maybe I redesign it entirely.

But at least now I'm choosing intentionally.

I don't trust "because."

I trust explanations.

I trust evidence.

I trust people who can tell me not just what works, but why.

Because once you understand the why, the next problem usually gets a little easier to solve.

And sooner or later, someone else is going to ask:

> Why are we doing it this way?

I hope they don't settle for:

> Because.
